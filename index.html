Understanding Different Needs

To build trust, we must respect the different backgrounds of the people we meet. Click on the headings below to learn how to support each group safely.

Men's and Women's Business

In many First Nations cultures, some topics are private. This is based on gender.

  • The Challenge: A man (whether a customer, patron, or colleague) might not feel comfortable talking about personal family matters with a female worker. Similarly, a woman might not talk to a male worker.
  • What to do: This is not them being difficult. It is a sign of respect for their culture. Always ask if they would prefer to speak to someone of the same gender.

Fear of Judgement

People who identify as LGBTQAI+ may worry that you will judge their relationship or identity.

  • The Challenge: They might fear you will say their relationship is not "real" or that they deserve the abuse because of their lifestyle.
  • What to do: Do not assume everyone is heterosexual (straight). Use the words they use to describe their partner. Show them respect for who they are.

Faith and Family

Religion can shape how a person sees marriage, family, and violence.

  • The Challenge: A person might feel that leaving a marriage is a sin against their religion, even if they are being hurt. They might fear you will tell them to break their religious rules.
  • What to do: Respect their faith. Listen to how their religion affects their choices. Do not tell them their religion is "wrong."

Independence and Dignity

Older people grew up in a time when family issues were kept very private.

  • The Challenge: They might feel shame about abuse. They are often afraid that if they speak up, they will lose their independence and be forced into a nursing home.
  • What to do: Be patient and respectful (consider using titles like Mr. or Mrs.). Assure them that your goal is to help them feel safe, not to control their life.

Trust and Authority

Young people are often wary of adults in positions of power.

  • The Challenge: They might worry that if they tell the truth, they will get in trouble or be separated from their family. They may not know the right words to use.
  • What to do: Listen to them seriously. Do not dismiss their feelings just because they are young. Speak simply and honestly about how you can help.
